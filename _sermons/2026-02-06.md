You learn to do things by doing them.  It’s great if you can find a book or a
teacher to give you a guided path!  But even then, you have to walk the path:
write the notes, struggle with the homework, test yourself.  That is true if
you’re an undergraduate, but it’s also true if you are a researcher headed into
a new area.  It’s possible to avoid a great deal of pain by realizing early on
that there is no magic here, just a steady investment of effort over time.  You
all teach, and you don’t expect your students to magically start knowing stuff
after reading a chapter once – and you have probably met a student or two who
found it surprising that this doesn’t work.  Nothing changes when you’re older
and struggling to learn a new application area or a new corner of mathematics,
except that you might not have the structure of a class to keep you honest any
more.
 
Confusion is the sweat of learning.  Don’t fear it.
